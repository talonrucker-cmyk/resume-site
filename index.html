The why

I chose finance because of the role models I grew up watching. My family carried very little stress about the future—and it was never luck, it was a plan. We thought early about retirement, insurance, and investing, and I watched those decisions quietly shape how we lived: with confidence instead of worry.

Once you've watched compound growth work on real money, it's hard not to want to share it. That's the whole reason I'm here.

Leading a sales team in college, I sat across from hundreds of families. One pattern stuck: the people genuinely at peace weren't always the ones earning the most—they were the ones with a plan that was simple, repeatable, and reliable. That's the difference I want to make.

Money is like oxygen. When it's flowing to the right places, you have freedom. When it gets disorganized, it's suffocating.

I'm a systems guy. I like taking the things that feel overwhelming and making them simple—so you can stop managing money and start using it to focus on what actually matters to you.

Cash flow concepts

Interrupt the spending circuit.

Rewire your paycheck so saving happens first, automatically.

See moreSee less ▾

Most Americans wire their whole paycheck straight into checking—so spending happens by default and saving only happens if there's willpower left over. Flip the wiring: pay your future self first, automatically, before the money ever touches checking.

The typical setup
⚡ Income

Lifestyle
Save? Only if there's money left over.
  • Lifestyle is plugged straight into income
  • Spend by default, save only by decision
  • A quiet, phantom drain on income over time

This is where most paychecks land: direct-deposited into checking, where whatever you didn't spend is what you "saved." For most people, that isn't much.

Automate the save first—15–20% swept into a wealth-building account before it ever reaches checking. It takes the pain (and the willpower) out of saving, and quietly compounds in the background.

    Term vs. whole life insurance.

    The one you asked about—what the two curves actually mean.

    See moreSee less ▾
    Why it matters: These are the two families of life insurance, and they solve different problems. Term is cheap, temporary protection for a set window; whole life costs more but is permanent and builds cash value you can use later.

    Term Life

    Death benefitCost
    • Covers a set number of years, then ends
    • Cheap early, gets pricier with age
    • No cash value—pure protection

    Whole Life

    Death benefitCash valueCost
    • Never expires—covers your whole life
    • Level cost that never rises
    • Builds cash value you can borrow/withdraw
    Explainer

    Disability: group vs. individual.

    Why the coverage through work often isn’t enough.

    See moreSee less ▾
    Why it matters: Your ability to earn is your biggest asset. Group coverage at work is a start, but it usually pays less, is taxable, and disappears when you change jobs. Knowing the difference is how you avoid a nasty surprise.

    Group (through work)

    • Benefit is taxable
    • Base salary only
    • Often pays only if fully disabled
    • Not customizable
    • Caps on the benefit
    • Ends when you leave the job

    Individual (your own)

    • Benefit is tax-free (paid with after-tax dollars)
    • Can include bonus & commissions
    • Covers partial disability
    • Customizable terms
    • Portable—follows you
    • Locks in while you’re healthy
